Slide 21 is our transition into the China market section. What we want to do here is simple: before we talk about product solutions, we first need to ground ourselves in what is actually happening in the market, because our launch strategy only works if it is built around real shifts in player behavior.

On slide 22, the key message is that China’s games market is still growing, but it is no longer growing in the old way. In 2025, domestic game revenue reached RMB 350.8 billion, up 7.68% year over year, and users reached 683 million. So this is still a very large and healthy market. But the real story is that growth is no longer coming from easy user acquisition. It is now coming from better live operations, higher-quality launches, cross-platform interoperability, and new content formats. That matters for us, because Fortnite is not entering China as just another new game. Its real advantage is that it combines several capabilities the market increasingly values, but still lacks in one single product.

On slide 23, the message becomes more specific: shooters are already one of the strongest genres in China, so we should not enter by simply calling Fortnite a shooter. In the 2025 top 100 domestic mobile games by revenue, shooter already accounts for 18.29% of total revenue, making it the number two genre right behind MOBA at 19.45%. That tells us Chinese players are already deeply familiar with shooter gameplay, and the market is already very crowded. So the opportunity is not to educate the market on shooters. The opportunity is to position Fortnite as something that can use shooting as the entry point, but expand beyond shooting as its long-term identity.

On slide 24, the story is about timing. If we look at the history of the shooter market, a major gameplay breakthrough tends to arrive every five to eight years. Round-based competitive shooters defined one era, battle royale defined the next, and now extraction is becoming the next major wave. The point of this page is that the market is not static. It is moving through clear cycles, and China is now entering another transition moment. That makes this a much better entry window for Fortnite than a stable market would be.

On slide 25, we show that shift visually. In 2024, battle royale still held 43% of genre share, while extraction was only 14%. By 2025, battle royale falls to 36%, and extraction jumps to 33%. That is the core takeaway from this page: extraction has moved from a niche subgenre into a mainstream force, and it is now approaching battle royale in scale. So if we want to align Fortnite with where the market is going, not where it has been, this is the direction we need to pay attention to.

On slide 26, we translate that data into a stronger conclusion. Battle royale was the format that educated the market, but extraction is now driving the next growth wave in survival shooters. The biggest movement here is the nineteen-point jump in extraction share, from 14% to 33%, while battle royale declines by seven points. So the story is not that battle royale is dead. The story is that player excitement, monetization momentum, and innovation energy are now shifting into extraction. That is why we believe Fortnite should not fight head-on inside an aging battle royale frame. It should absorb what the market is learning from extraction and use that as a new way in.

On slide 27, we show how the top products are already doing exactly that. Game for Peace remains the category leader not by staying pure, but by adding Metro Royale’s extraction loop into its ecosystem. At the same time, total extraction DAU reaches 55 million in 2025, with Metro Royale at 37 million, Hazard Operations at 15 million, and Arena Breakout at 10 million. The story here is very important: in China, the winning products are not standing still inside one mode. They are layering new gameplay loops onto an existing base. That is highly relevant for Fortnite, because modular expansion is already one of its natural strengths.

On slide 28, the message shifts from genre to platform. Mobile is still the core of the China market, accounting for 73.3% of total revenue, but PC client revenue has reached 22.3% and is growing much faster, up 14.97% year over year. So the right way to read this page is not “mobile versus PC.” It is that China is now a mobile-led market in which PC is regaining strategic importance, especially for higher-value players. That creates a real tailwind for Fortnite, because Fortnite is structurally built for cross-platform play rather than single-platform dependence.

On slide 29, we go one step further and compare revenue scale with audience scale by genre. Battle royale still has the largest estimated DAU at 90 million, while extraction is lower at 55 million. But in estimated revenue, extraction actually reaches RMB 16.5 billion, slightly above battle royale at RMB 15 billion. That is the real story of this page. Extraction is already monetizing at a higher value density than battle royale, especially because of its stronger PC contribution. So if we only look at user scale, we miss the point. The more important signal is that extraction is becoming a very high-value segment.

On slides 30 and 31, we stop summarizing and answer the strategic question directly: where should Fortnite enter? Our answer is that we should not keep fighting in the crowded traditional shooter market. Instead, we should win through three shifts. First, a perception shift, from a hardcore competitive shooter into a youth entertainment and social experience. Second, a genre shift, from competing on pure shooter development to competing through extensible gameplay and a UGC-driven ecosystem. Third, a platform shift, from a PC-led cross-platform model into a mobile-first three-platform experience. In other words, our market entry is not about joining the old lane better. It is about choosing a different lane entirely.

Slide 32 is the payoff line. If the previous pages explain the market logic, this page turns that logic into positioning. Our proposal is to present Fortnite in China not as another shooter, and not even just as another game, but as China’s first living universe of interactive culture. That positioning is important because it captures all three advantages at once: gameplay variety, cross-platform behavior, and creator-led content expansion. It gives us a larger and more ownable story than “battle royale plus building,” which is exactly the perception trap we need to escape.
